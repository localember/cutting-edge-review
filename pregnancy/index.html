The short answer
Pregnancy changes the mechanics of your spine and pelvis quickly: weight redistributes forward, ligaments soften, and the low back takes the difference. Chiropractic care works on that mechanical side, easing joints that have stiffened under a load they did not have three months ago, with positioning and force adapted to where you are in the pregnancy.
Pregnancy back pain gets waved off more than almost anything else we see, usually with some version of "well, you are pregnant." That is true and it is not a plan. Here is a plain-language look at what is actually changing, how chiropractic care can help, and what is different about care during pregnancy.

What causes back pain in pregnancy?

It is not simply the weight. Three things change at once. Your centre of gravity shifts forward, so the low back has to work harder just to hold you upright. Relaxin softens the ligaments across the pelvis in preparation for birth, which makes those joints looser and less stable. And the abdominal muscles that normally share the load stretch and stop pulling their weight. The low back and pelvis absorb all three.
What tends to make it worse:
  • Standing or walking for long stretches, especially later in the day.
  • A job that keeps you on your feet, or one that keeps you in a chair.
  • An older child who still wants carrying, usually on one hip.
  • Sleep that has become a negotiation with pillows and positions.
  • A low back that was already grumbling before the pregnancy started.
Some things are not mechanical and need your doctor or midwife first, not us: bleeding, regular tightening or cramping, fluid, a sharp drop in movement from the baby, or severe pain that came on suddenly. Part of a good first visit is knowing which is which, and we would rather send you than see you.

How does chiropractic care help in pregnancy?

Chiropractic care works on the mechanics of what has changed. Gentle, specific adjustments restore movement to the joints of the low back and pelvis that have stiffened and guarded under the new load, and hands-on work eases the muscle that has been compensating. Everything is adapted: the positioning, the force, and the plan, all matched to your trimester.
In practice, a course of care usually blends a few things:
  • Gentle adjustments to the low back and pelvis, with positioning that works with a bump rather than around it.
  • Soft-tissue work through the hips and low back that are carrying the change.
  • Movement you can actually do, adjusted as the pregnancy goes on.
  • Practical help with sleeping positions, sitting, and getting in and out of a car without bracing.
The goal is not a lifetime of visits. It is to get you through this comfortably, and to leave you with the movement and habits that help afterwards too.
